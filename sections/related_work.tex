\chapter{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\section{Distillation}

Knowledge distillation was introduced as a method for transferring the behavior of a large model or ensemble into a smaller model by training the student to match softened teacher predictions \cite{hintonDistillingKnowledgeNeural2015}. In the language-modeling setting, this basic idea has been extended along several axes: scaling distillation systems to large pretrained language models \cite{tan2023GKDGeneralKnowledge}, reducing deployment cost through online distillation in large-scale pretraining \cite{wang2021ERNIE30Titan}, improving token selection through selective token-weighted objectives \cite{huang2025SelecTKDSelectiveTokenWeighted}, and enabling distillation across models with incompatible tokenizers \cite{boizardCrossTokenizerDistillationUniversal2025,minixhoferUniversalCrossTokenizerDistillation2025}. For reasoning models, sequence-level and chain-of-thought distillation have also become central, with recent work identifying limitations in standard supervised fine-tuning pipelines for long-CoT reasoning and proposing distribution-aligned sequence distillation to better match the teacher's sequence-level behavior \cite{yanDistributionAlignedSequenceDistillation2026}.

Most classical distillation methods are naturally \emph{off-policy}: the student is trained on a fixed data distribution, usually consisting of human-written examples, teacher-generated solutions, or teacher-labeled prefixes. In this setting, the teacher is queried on prefixes $y_{<t}$ from a dataset rather than prefixes generated by the current student policy. This family includes both soft-label distillation and supervised fine-tuning on teacher-written traces. Off-policy approaches are attractive because they are simple, stable, and easy to cache. However, they do not directly train the student on the states it will visit at inference time. This mismatch is especially important for autoregressive reasoning, where early student mistakes can move the model into prefixes that are unlikely under the teacher or the training data.

\section{On-policy distillation}
On-policy distillation addresses this distribution mismatch by sampling continuations from the student and asking the teacher to provide supervision at the student's own prefixes. Generalized Knowledge Distillation trains language models from self-generated sequences with teacher feedback, showing that on-policy data can improve summarization, translation, arithmetic reasoning, and instruction tuning \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024}. MiniLLM similarly argues that forward KL can cause student models to overestimate low-probability regions of the teacher distribution, and instead proposes an on-policy reverse-KL formulation that improves precision, calibration, exposure bias, and long-form generation quality \cite{guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026}. A broader technical discussion of on-policy distillation for language models is given in \cite{luOnPolicyDistillation2025}.

The distinction between off-policy and on-policy distillation is central to this thesis, because on-policy distillation changes the teacher's role from producing complete demonstrations to giving local guidance on states induced by the student. Recent analyses suggest that this distinction matters. On-policy data can mitigate forgetting because it preserves the model's own behavioral distribution during learning \cite{chenRetainingDoingRole2025}, and diagnostic work shows that on-policy distillation is most useful precisely when the student rollout is wrong, while the best distillation context depends on both task and student capacity \cite{armandpourUnmaskingOnPolicyDistillation2026}. These results motivate studying on-policy teacher feedback for mathematical reasoning, where student errors often create prefixes that are absent from teacher-written solution traces.

\section{The teacher--student capacity gap.}
A separate line of work studies the \emph{capacity gap}: the empirical observation that a much stronger teacher is not always a better teacher for a much smaller student. Early work on teacher assistants showed that student performance can degrade when the teacher is too large relative to the student, and proposed intermediate teacher-assistant models to bridge the gap \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019}. Similar ideas appear in progressive distillation, where the student is guided along an optimized path from its initial distribution toward the teacher rather than being forced to imitate the teacher directly \cite{shi2021FollowYourPath}. In language models, MiniMoE addresses the ``curse of capacity gap'' by augmenting the student with minimal expert modules that improve transfer without greatly increasing inference cost \cite{zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}. TAID instead constructs a temporally adaptive intermediate distribution between student and teacher, gradually shifting the target as the student improves \cite{shingTAIDTemporallyAdaptive2025}.

More recent work addresses the capacity gap in ways that are particularly relevant to generative language models. Speculative Knowledge Distillation lets the student propose tokens while the teacher corrects or replaces low-quality choices, thereby producing training data that is closer to the student's inference-time distribution \cite{xu2024SpeculativeKnowledgeDistillation}. Veto reformulates on-policy distillation targets through a geometric bridge in logit space, suppressing harmful gradients when the student and teacher are too far apart \cite{jang2026StableOnPolicyDistillation}. In mathematical reasoning, the capacity gap is especially pronounced: small models may fail to benefit from long chain-of-thought traces produced by strong reasoners, and can perform better when trained on shorter or mixed-complexity reasoning traces aligned with their own capacity \cite{liSmallModelsStruggle2025}. These works establish that the capacity gap is not merely a matter of teacher quality; it is also a matter of whether the training objective exposes information that the student can actually use.

This thesis builds on that observation but studies a different intervention. Rather than inserting intermediate teacher models, changing the student architecture, or shortening teacher traces, the goal is to test whether a very large teacher can still provide useful signal when queried on the student's own prefixes and when the loss preserves more of the teacher's local distribution than top-1 hard labels. This is motivated by the possibility that the apparent capacity gap is partly an \emph{objective bottleneck}: a large teacher may contain useful information, but standard hard-label or sequence-level distillation may hide that information from the student.

\section{Privileged context and self-distillation.}
Privileged-context distillation gives the teacher information that is unavailable to the student. This is related to learning using privileged information, but recent language-model work has adapted the idea to agentic and reasoning settings. $\pi$-Distill trains a privileged-information-conditioned teacher and an unconditioned student jointly, while On-Policy Self-Distillation uses a reverse-KL penalty between the student and a privileged teacher in multi-turn agentic environments \cite{penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026}. On-Policy Context Distillation combines on-policy distillation with context distillation by training the student on its own trajectories while matching a teacher conditioned on additional context, with applications to experiential knowledge, system prompt distillation, math reasoning, games, and domain-specific tasks \cite{yeOnPolicyContextDistillation2026}. Self-Distilled Reasoner uses a single model as both teacher and student by conditioning the teacher role on verified reasoning traces while leaving the student role unconditioned, achieving token-efficient improvements on mathematical reasoning benchmarks \cite{zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026}.

Several related methods use self-distillation to convert richer feedback into dense token-level supervision. Self-Distilled RLVR shows that directly using a privileged teacher conditioned on reference answers can cause information leakage and unstable training, and therefore combines self-distillation magnitudes with RLVR update directions \cite{yangSelfDistilledRLVR2026,yangSelfDistilledRLVR2026a}. Self-Distillation Fine-Tuning uses a demonstration-conditioned version of the model as an in-context teacher to support continual learning while reducing forgetting \cite{shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}. Self-Distillation Policy Optimization conditions the model on textual feedback such as runtime errors or judge evaluations, then distills the feedback-informed predictions back into the policy \cite{hubotterReinforcementLearningSelfDistillation2026}. SD-Zero similarly turns binary rewards into dense supervision by training a generator from a reward-conditioned reviser \cite{heSelfDistillationZeroSelfRevision2026}. These methods demonstrate the value of privileged or feedback-conditioned teachers, but they typically focus on self-distillation or relatively matched teacher--student capacities.

Self-distillation can also have failure modes in reasoning domains. In particular, self-distillation may shorten responses while degrading mathematical reasoning by suppressing epistemic verbalization, especially when the teacher is conditioned on rich information that reduces its visible uncertainty \cite{kimWhyDoesSelfDistillation2026}. This suggests that privileged context is not automatically beneficial: the distillation objective must preserve useful uncertainty and local alternatives rather than collapsing the teacher's behavior into a single polished trace or answer.
