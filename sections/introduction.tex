\chapter{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly capable general-purpose systems. They can solve a wide range of tasks, adapt to new instructions, and provide useful behavior across domains without requiring task-specific training for every new application. However, this generality comes at a substantial cost because the strongest models are often large, computationally expensive to serve, and slow relative to the requirements of many real-world deployment settings. Even when a large model performs well, using it directly at inference time may be impractical because every query requires substantial memory, compute, and energy.

In many applications, we do not need the full generality of a large language model. Instead, we want a model that is highly capable on a narrower distribution of tasks, such as a domain-specific assistant, a task-focused reasoner, or a model optimized for a particular interaction pattern. For those settings, the ideal system is not necessarily the most general model available, but rather one that preserves the relevant capabilities while being cheap, fast, and reliable to run. This motivates distillation because it enables using a powerful teacher model to transfer useful behavior into a smaller student model \cite{hintonDistillingKnowledgeNeural2015}. In the best case, distillation allows us to compress the skills of the most capable model into a specialized model that is much more efficient at inference time.

The appeal of distillation is strongest when the student is as small as possible because smaller students are faster to run, easier to deploy, and cheaper to serve. At the same time, the most desirable teachers are usually the largest models available since model capability often increases with scale. The difference between the number of parameters of the student and the teacher is called the capacity gap because it represent a difference in representational and computational power. A large capacity gap is attractive because it promises the greatest efficiency gain: the student is small, while the teacher provides high-quality supervision. However, it also makes the distillation problem substantially harder because the student cannot match the capabilities of a teacher \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}.

This difficulty gives rise to what is often called the curse of the capacity gap \cite{zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}. When the teacher and student are too different in capacity, the student may fail to benefit from the teacher’s supervision. In extreme cases, a larger, more capable teacher can lead to worse student performance than a smaller, less capable one \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, liSmallModelsStruggle2025}. This is counterintuitive: one might expect the best teacher to always provide the best training signal. However, if the teacher’s behavior is too complex, too sharp, or too far from what the student can represent, the supervision may become difficult for the student to learn effectively. As a result, the teacher’s additional capability does not translate into better student performance.

This creates a central challenge for distillation. The models we most want to distill from are often the largest and most capable, but those are precisely the ones that may introduce the most severe capacity gap. If we restrict ourselves to smaller teachers, distillation becomes easier but less desirable because the student inherits behavior from a teacher that is not the best at the task. If we restrict to larger students, we may obtain more capable models at the cost of higher inference costs. To fully realize the promise of distillation, we need methods that enable small students to learn effectively from much larger teachers \cite{shi2021FollowYourPath, shingTAIDTemporallyAdaptive2025, jang2026StableOnPolicyDistillation}. Bridging the capacity gap would allow us to combine the strengths of both sides: the capability of large models and the efficiency of small specialized models.

This thesis studies knowledge distillation under extreme capacity gaps to identify the best ways to extract knowledge from large teachers to small students. In particular, we investigate settings where the teacher can be up to 400 times larger than the student because such extreme capacity gaps are becoming increasingly common with the most capable models reaching 1 trillion parameters \cite{team2026KimiK2Open, deepseekai2026deepseekv4}.


First, we show the effectiveness of on-policy distillation (OPD) compared to off-policy distillation in extreme capacity gaps by using the rollouts from the student and using the log-probabilities from the teacher to provide feedback \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024, guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026, luOnPolicyDistillation2025}. We examine what is required for OPD to remain effective in such extreme student-teacher settings by studying which factors matter when the teacher is far larger than the student such as top-k losses and different types of privileged information seen only by the teacher \cite{penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026, zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026, shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}.

Second, we explore the best way to encourage token efficiency in the math reasoning domain. Besides using a small model, making the model answer in fewer tokens is also a source of efficiency when deploying the model, so we show the best way to encourage that behavior in different sizes of capacity gaps. We compare the context distillation and privileged context techniques \cite{yeOnPolicyContextDistillation2026, sang2026CRISPCompressedReasoning} and compare them to using a system prompt directly passed to the student.

Third, we show how each technique impacts catastrophic forgetting by measuring model performance in domains that we do not target during training \cite{chenRetainingDoingRole2025, shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}. While distillation aims to improve a student model on a specialized task distribution, this improvement may come at the cost of degrading capabilities outside that target domain \cite{kimWhyDoesSelfDistillation2026}. This trade-off is especially important for AI models, where even specialized systems may still be expected to retain general linguistic, reasoning, or instruction-following abilities. By evaluating performance on non-target domains, we can assess whether a given distillation method merely transfers task-specific behavior or whether it does so while preserving broader model competence. This allows us to study not only how well each technique bridges the capacity gap, but also how safely it specializes the student without erasing useful prior knowledge.

Finally, studying and working on this regime requires addressing both methodological and systems challenges. To make this possible, we implement efficient training procedures that allow large teachers to be used during OPD at this scale. We make our approach and lessons available to the community as a trainer\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/docs/trl/distillation_trainer}} in TRL \cite{vonwerra2020trl} and as a blog post \cite{patino2026_distilling_100b_models_40x_faster_with_trl} that others can follow when they encounter similar engineering challenges.

To summarize, our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Show how OPD distillation performs better than off-policy distillation across different capacity gaps (Section \ref{sec: exps_off_vs_on}). Additionally, we show how using privileged context is not an effective solution to bridge all capacity gaps and that using top-k in large capacity gaps degrade the student's performance (Section \ref{sec: exps_top_k}).
    \item Comparing on-policy context distillation to using a system prompt during training and showing how context distillation introduces training dynamics that ultimately degrade model performance (Section \ref{sec: exps_system_prompt}).
    \item \red{Catastrophic forgetting results} (Section \ref{sec: exps_forgetting}).
    \item Developing an efficient trainer that is available to the AI community to leverage the benefits of OPD at different capacity gaps.
\end{itemize}

Together, these contributions aim to better understand the limits of distillation and to expand the range of settings in which small models can successfully learn from much larger, more capable teachers.
