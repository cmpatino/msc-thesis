\chapter{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly capable general-purpose systems. They can solve a wide range of tasks, adapt to new instructions, and provide useful behavior across domains without requiring task-specific training for every new application. However, this generality comes at a substantial cost because the strongest models are often large, computationally expensive to serve, and slow relative to the requirements of many real-world deployment settings. Even when a large model performs well, using it directly at inference time may be impractical because every query requires substantial memory, compute, and energy.

In many applications, we do not need the full generality of a large language model. Instead, we want a model that is highly capable on a narrower distribution of tasks, such as a domain-specific assistant, a task-focused reasoner, or a model optimized for a particular interaction pattern. For those settings, the ideal system is not necessarily the most general model available, but rather one that preserves the relevant capabilities while being cheap, fast, and reliable to run. This motivates distillation because it enables using a powerful teacher model to transfer useful behavior into a smaller student model \cite{hintonDistillingKnowledgeNeural2015}. In the best case, distillation allows us to compress the skills of the most capable model into a specialized model that is much more efficient at inference time.

The appeal of distillation is strongest when the student is as small as possible because smaller students are faster to run, easier to deploy, and cheaper to serve. At the same time, the most desirable teachers are usually the largest models available since model capability often increases with scale. This ideal scenario then has a large difference in the number of parameters between the teacher and the student. This difference between the number of parameters is \textit{called the capacity gap} because it represent a difference in representational and computational power. A large capacity gap is attractive because it promises the greatest efficiency gain: the student is small, while the teacher provides high-quality supervision. However, it also makes the distillation problem substantially harder because the student cannot match the capabilities of a teacher \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}.

This difficulty gives rise to what is often called the curse of the capacity gap \cite{zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}. When the teacher and student are too different in capacity, the student may fail to benefit from the teacher’s supervision. In extreme cases, a larger, more capable teacher can lead to worse student performance than a smaller, less capable one \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, liSmallModelsStruggle2025}. This is counterintuitive: one might expect the best teacher to always provide the best training signal. However, if the teacher’s behavior is too complex, too sharp, or too far from what the student can represent, the supervision may become difficult for the student to learn effectively. As a result, the teacher’s additional capability does not translate into better student performance.

This creates a central challenge for distillation. The models we most want to distill from are often the largest and most capable, but those are precisely the ones that may introduce the most severe capacity gap. If we restrict ourselves to smaller teachers, distillation becomes easier but less desirable because the student inherits behavior from a teacher that is not the best at the task. If we restrict to larger students, we may obtain more capable models at the cost of higher inference costs. Hence, to fully realize the promise of distillation, we need methods that enable small students to learn effectively from much larger teachers \cite{shi2021FollowYourPath, shingTAIDTemporallyAdaptive2025, jang2026StableOnPolicyDistillation}. Bridging the capacity gap would allow us to combine the strengths of both sides: the capability of large models and the efficiency of small specialized models.

This thesis studies knowledge distillation under extreme teacher–student capacity gaps, with the goal of identifying effective ways to transfer knowledge from large teachers to much smaller students. In particular, we investigate settings in which the teacher can be up to 400 times larger than the student, motivated by the fact that such extreme capacity gaps are becoming increasingly common as the most capable models approach trillion-scale parameter counts \cite{team2026KimiK2Open, deepseekai2026deepseekv4, glm-5-team2026GLM5VibeCoding}. We focus on on-policy distillation (OPD) \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024, guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026, luOnPolicyDistillation2025} with privileged context (PC) \cite{penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026, zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026, shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}, as this combination is especially promising for addressing large capacity gaps. OPD queries the teacher on states visited by the student, so it reduces the exposure bias that may become more severe as the student’s behavior diverges from that of a much larger teacher. PC provides a complementary benefit because larger teachers are expected to have stronger in-context learning capabilities. As the capacity gap grows, the teacher’s ability to exploit additional context should also increase. Privileged context can therefore leverage the teacher’s growing in-context learning advantage to produce richer and more informative supervision for the smaller student.

We study OPD under extreme teacher--student capacity gaps and test several ways of making the teacher signal more useful to the student. First, we compare OPD with off-policy distillation and show that on-policy supervision is consistently stronger in our mathematical reasoning setting. However, we also find that OPD alone does not solve the capacity gap: increasing the teacher size from 4B or 30B to 235B does not reliably improve the student and can reduce the fraction of the teacher--student gap that is recovered.

Second, we evaluate privileged context as a way to exploit the teacher's stronger in-context learning ability. Our results show that this mechanism is fragile. A short PC that informs the teacher about the distillation setup already changes downstream performance, but it does not improve capacity-gap recovery. A more targeted PC for concise reasoning is also less effective than giving the same instruction directly to the student through a system prompt. This suggests that, in on-policy distillation, it is not enough for the teacher to see the right instruction at scoring time; the student must also generate rollouts under the behavior we want it to learn.

Third, we test whether privileged context can emulate teacher-assistant distillation inside a single OPD run. Instead of training through multiple intermediate teachers, we give a smaller scoring teacher access to rollouts from a larger teacher. This does not improve transfer and can sharply degrade performance, suggesting that a smaller capacity gap is not enough if the bridge teacher lacks the in-context ability to turn the larger teacher's rollout into useful token-level feedback.

Fourth, we analyze the failures induced by large teachers. We show that truncation is one of the dominant error modes, especially for the 0.6B student trained with larger teachers. We also find that teacher scaling substantially increases rollout length, while the number of epistemic words does not scale in the same way under brevity instructions. This separates two phenomena that are often conflated: longer traces are not simply the result of more explicit epistemic self-talk, but appear to be another symptom of the student struggling to absorb feedback from a much larger teacher.

Fifth, we evaluate whether the math-focused gains come at the cost of broader behavior. On GPQA-Diamond, the 0.6B student loses out-of-distribution scientific reasoning performance as the teacher size increases, while the 4B student remains stable or slightly improves. This suggests that catastrophic forgetting under OPD is also capacity dependent: the smallest student can recover target-domain reasoning while sacrificing capabilities outside the training distribution.

Sixth, we test whether richer token-level feedback can mitigate the gap by extending OPD from top-1 to top-$K$ teacher log probabilities. Although increasing $K$ improves probability mass coverage, it does not translate into better AIME performance under large capacity gaps. The 0.6B student benefits from larger $K$ only when trained with the nearby 4B teacher; with 30B and 235B teachers, additional log probabilities leave performance unchanged or make it worse.

Finally, studying this regime requires addressing systems challenges that do not appear in ordinary supervised distillation. OPD must generate fresh student rollouts inside the training loop, query large teachers for token-level feedback, and move large log-probability payloads between serving and training workers. To make these experiments possible, we implement efficient training procedures for large-teacher OPD and release them as a trainer\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/docs/trl/distillation_trainer}} in TRL \cite{vonwerra2020trl}, together with a blog post \cite{patino2026_distilling_100b_models_40x_faster_with_trl} documenting the engineering lessons.

The main scientific novelty of this thesis is to separate teacher strength from teacher-signal usability in on-policy distillation. Prior work studies on-policy distillation, privileged-context distillation, and the capacity-gap problem, but this thesis studies their intersection under extreme teacher--student capacity gaps for mathematical reasoning. The novel question is not simply whether a larger teacher is better, but whether the teacher's signal remains usable for a much smaller student when supervision is provided on the student's own rollouts. The results show that OPD helps relative to off-policy distillation, but also that larger teachers, teacher-only privileged context, and richer top-$K$ teacher distributions do not automatically produce better students. Instead, successful distillation depends on aligning the teacher signal with the student's capacity and with the rollout distribution the student actually visits during training.

To summarize, our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Showing that OPD outperforms off-policy distillation across extreme capacity gaps, but that larger teachers do not reliably produce better students or higher gap recovery (Sections \ref{sec: exp_opd_and_pc} and \ref{sec: exps_off_vs_on}).
    \item Showing that privileged context is not a general solution to the capacity gap: simple teacher-only context can degrade performance, and targeted context distillation is less effective than directly conditioning the student with a system prompt (Section \ref{sec: exps_system_prompt}).
    \item Showing that teacher-assisted privileged context does not solve the capacity gap: routing a larger teacher's rollout through a smaller scoring teacher can degrade performance, indicating that bridge teachers must be capable of using the hidden context effectively (Section \ref{sec: exps_teacher_assisted_pc}).
    \item Identifying truncation and excessive rollout length as central failure modes when small students learn from larger teachers, and showing that rollout-length growth is not explained solely by increased epistemic wording (Sections \ref{sec: exps_system_prompt} and \ref{sec: exps_rollout_lengths}).
    \item Showing that target-domain gains can come with out-of-distribution degradation: the 0.6B student reaches roughly its reasoning-mode AIME performance but loses GPQA-Diamond performance as the teacher grows, while the 4B student retains more out-of-distribution reasoning (Section \ref{sec: exps_catastrophic_ood}).
    \item Showing that increasing from top-1 to top-$K$ teacher log probabilities improves probability mass coverage but can worsen student performance under large capacity gaps (Section \ref{sec: exps_top_k}).
    \item Identifying a capacity effect inside the capacity gap: even under similar teacher--student ratios and AIME gaps, the 4B student recovers substantially more of the gap than the 0.6B student, suggesting that absolute student capacity affects teacher-signal usability (Section \ref{sec: exps_capacity_effect}).
    \item Developing and releasing an efficient TRL trainer for large-teacher OPD, enabling on-policy rollout generation and remote teacher scoring at the scale required for these experiments.
\end{itemize}

Together, these contributions aim to better understand the limits of distillation and to expand the range of settings in which small models can successfully learn from much larger, more capable teachers.
