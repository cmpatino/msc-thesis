\chapter{Introduction}

Large language models (LLMs) have become increasingly capable general-purpose systems. They can solve a wide range of tasks, adapt to new instructions, and provide useful behavior across domains without requiring task-specific training for every new application. However, this generality comes at a substantial cost. The strongest models are often large, computationally expensive to serve, and slow relative to the requirements of many real-world deployment settings. Even when a large model performs well, using it directly at inference time may be impractical because every query requires substantial memory, compute, and energy.

In many applications, we do not need the full generality of a large language model. Instead, we want a model that is highly capable on a narrower distribution of tasks, such as a domain-specific assistant, a task-focused reasoner, or a model optimized for a particular interaction pattern. For those settings, the ideal system is not necessarily the most general model available, but rather one that preserves the relevant capabilities while being cheap, fast, and reliable to run. This motivates knowledge distillation: using a powerful teacher model to transfer useful behavior into a smaller student model \cite{hintonDistillingKnowledgeNeural2015}. In the best case, distillation allows us to compress the skills of a capable model into a specialized model that is much more efficient at inference time.

The appeal of distillation is strongest when the student is as small as possible. Smaller students are faster to run, easier to deploy, and cheaper to serve. At the same time, the most desirable teachers are usually the most capable models available. Since model capability often increases with scale, the teacher may have many more parameters than the student. This difference in representational and computational capacity defines the capacity gap between teacher and student. A large capacity gap is attractive because it promises the greatest efficiency gain: the student is small, while the teacher provides high-quality supervision. But it also makes the distillation problem substantially harder \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}.

This difficulty gives rise to what is often called the curse of the capacity gap \cite{zhangLiftingCurseCapacity2023}. When the teacher and student are too different in capacity, the student may fail to benefit from the teacher’s supervision. In extreme cases, a larger, more capable teacher can lead to worse student performance than a smaller, less capable one \cite{mirzadehImprovedKnowledgeDistillation2019, liSmallModelsStruggle2025}. This is counterintuitive: one might expect the best teacher to always provide the best training signal. However, if the teacher’s behavior is too complex, too sharp, or too far from what the student can represent, the supervision may become difficult for the student to imitate effectively. As a result, the teacher’s additional capability does not translate into better student performance.

This creates a central challenge for distillation. The models we most want to distill from are often the largest and most capable, but those are precisely the ones that may introduce the most severe capacity gap. If we restrict ourselves to smaller teachers, distillation becomes easier but less ambitious: the student can only inherit behavior from a teacher with limited capability. If we restrict to larger students, we may obtain more capable models at the cost of higher inference costs. To fully realize the promise of distillation, we need methods that enable small students to learn effectively from much larger teachers \cite{shi2021FollowYourPath, shingTAIDTemporallyAdaptive2025, jang2026StableOnPolicyDistillation}. Bridging the capacity gap would allow us to combine the strengths of both sides: the capability of large models and the efficiency of small specialized models.

This thesis studies knowledge distillation under extreme capacity gaps. In particular, we investigate settings where the teacher can be up to 400 times larger than the student. Studying this regime requires addressing both methodological and systems challenges. Large teachers are expensive to serve during training, especially when distillation is performed on-policy \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024}, where the student’s own generations are used to construct the learning signal. To make this possible, we implement efficient training procedures that allow large teachers to be used during on-policy distillation OPD at this scale. We make our approach and lessons available to the community as a trainer in TRL \cite{vonwerra2020trl} and as a blogpost \cite{patino2026_distilling_100b_models_40x_faster_with_trl} that others can follow when they encounter similar engineering challenges.

Prior work has shown that OPD can outperform off-policy distillation because it trains the student on the kinds of inputs and outputs that arise from its own behavior \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024, guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026, luOnPolicyDistillation2025}. However, it remains unclear how this advantage behaves when the student and teacher differ dramatically in capacity \cite{armandpourUnmaskingOnPolicyDistillation2026, liRethinkingOnPolicyDistillation2026}. This thesis examines what is required for OPD to remain effective in such extreme student-teacher settings. We study which factors matter when the teacher is far larger than the student, and how the distillation process can be made robust despite the mismatch in capacity.

Additionally, this thesis explores the role of in-context learning on bridging the capacity gap. Specifically we use the in-context learning capabilities of the teacher model by using privileged context as a mechanism for bridging the capacity gap \cite{penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026, yeOnPolicyContextDistillation2026, zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026, shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}. Privileged context provides the teacher with additional information that is not necessarily available to the student at inference time, enabling the teacher to deliver richer, more targeted supervision during training. We investigate whether this additional context can make the teacher’s signal more useful to a much smaller student, and whether it can improve distillation when the gap between teacher and student is especially large. Together, these contributions aim to better understand the limits of distillation and to expand the range of settings in which small models can successfully learn from much larger, more capable teachers.

Finally, we also measure how each technique impacts catastrophic forgetting by measuring model performance in domains that we do not target during training \cite{chenRetainingDoingRole2025, shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026}. While distillation aims to improve a student model on a specialized task distribution, this improvement may come at the cost of degrading capabilities outside that target domain \cite{kimWhyDoesSelfDistillation2026}. This trade-off is especially important for language models, where even specialized systems may still be expected to retain general linguistic, reasoning, or instruction-following abilities. By evaluating performance on non-target domains, we can assess whether a given distillation method merely transfers task-specific behavior or whether it does so while preserving broader model competence. This allows us to study not only how well each technique bridges the capacity gap, but also how safely it specializes the student without erasing useful prior knowledge.
