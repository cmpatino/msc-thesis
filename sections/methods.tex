\section{Distillation Formalism} \label{sec:distillation_formalism}

Following the notation of on-policy generalized knowledge distillation \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024}, let $x$ denote a prompt, let $V$ denote the vocabulary, and let $y = (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_{L_y})$ denote an output sequence. The prefix before token $y_n$ is $y_{<n} = (y_1, \ldots, y_{n-1})$. We write the student policy as $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$ and the teacher policy as $\pi_T(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$, both of which define next-token distributions over $V$.

The simplest distillation objective treats a target sequence as a hard label and trains the student with cross-entropy. If $\mathcal{D}$ is a dataset of prompt--completion pairs, where the completions can be human-written or sampled from the teacher, the loss is

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(\theta)
= \mathrm{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}
\left[
- \sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\log \pi_\theta(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:xentropy}
\end{equation}

This objective only uses the observed target token $y_n$ at each position. We refer to this method as supervised fine-tuning (SFT) \cite{zotero-item-425}.

A richer form of distillation uses the full teacher distribution over the vocabulary instead of using the token sampled by the teacher. For a fixed prompt and completion, the token-level forward KL from teacher to student is

\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_y} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})}
{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})}.
\label{eq:forward_kl}
\end{equation}

The reverse KL swaps the order of the two distributions:

\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_y} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})}
{\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})}.
\label{eq:reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

The distinction is important because KL divergence is asymmetric. Forward KL averages the log-ratio under the teacher distribution, so it penalizes the student for failing to place probability mass on tokens the teacher considers likely. This makes it a mass-covering objective. Reverse KL averages under the student distribution, so it penalizes tokens the student samples when the teacher assigns them low probability. This makes it more mode-seeking and can be useful when a lower-capacity student cannot match the full teacher distribution.

We show the distinction graphically in Figure~\ref{fig:toy_forward_vs_reverse} using a plot from \cite{guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026} that shows how the forward and reverse KL fit a Gaussian mixture distribution with a single Gaussian distribution. The reverse KL seeks the major mode of the mixture while the forward KL tries to fit all three.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/forward_vs_reverse.pdf}
    \caption{Image from \cite{guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026} showing the difference of using forward and reverse KL to fit a mixture of three Gaussians. The reverse KL tries to fit the major mode while the forward KL tries to approximate the three modes.}
    \label{fig:toy_forward_vs_reverse}
\end{figure}

On top of the distinction between forward and reverse KL, it is important to distinguish where we sample the rollouts $y$ we use for training. Off-policy distillation samples the rollouts from the teacher policy $y \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$ and then seeks the feedback from the same teacher. On-policy distillation samples completions from the student and then compares teacher and student distributions on the prefixes that the student actually visits. We illustrate both settings in Figure~\ref{fig:off_vs_on_policy}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/off_vs_on_policy_distillation.png}
    \caption{Off-policy distillation generates completions from the teacher and trains the student on them. On-policy distillation generates completions from the student and scores them with both models.}
    \label{fig:off_vs_on_policy}
\end{figure}

The on-policy objective is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OPD}}(\theta)
= 
\mathrm{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}
\left[
D(\pi_T, \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
\right],
\label{eq:on_policy_distillation}
\end{equation}

where $D$ can be instantiated as the forward KL in Equation~\ref{eq:forward_kl}, the reverse KL in Equation~\ref{eq:reverse_kl}, or another token-level divergence. We can define off-policy distillation analogously by changing $y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ for $y \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$.

In practice, computing the full sums over $V$ can be expensive. A common solution is to The sampled-token reverse-KL formulation used in on-policy distillation keeps only the realized token at each position of a student rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$. The resulting top-1 sampled-token estimate is

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(\hat{y} \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:sampled_token_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

This is the sampled-token analogue of Equation~\ref{eq:reverse_kl}. Rather than matching the full next-token distributions, it scores the token actually produced by the student under both policies.

We can write an analogous formulation for the Equation~\ref{eq:forward_kl}, where we only use the realized token at each position of a teacher rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$. Additionally, we use the forward instead of the reverse KL to avoid optimizing a biased surrogate or using importance sampling to correct the off-policy nature of the data.

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(\hat{y} \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl}
\end{equation}

The formulation in Equation~\ref{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl} is equivalent to the soft version of SFT, because we are including the log probability of the token sampled by the teacher instead of excluding it and using it as a hard target as in Equation~\ref{eq:xentropy}.

\section{Top-K KL}

The KL objectives in Equations~\ref{eq:forward_kl} and~\ref{eq:reverse_kl} require summing over the full vocabulary at every token position. This is expensive for large language models, because the vocabulary can contain hundreds of thousands of tokens. A practical approximation is to keep the $k$ tokens with the largest log-probabilities under one of the two policies. However, we do not renormalize the retained log-probabilities over only those $k$ tokens. Renormalization would discard how much probability mass the model assigns to the rest of the vocabulary. Instead, we group all tokens outside the selected top-$k$ set into a single tail bucket.

For compactness, write $h_n = (x, y_{<n})$. For each prefix, define the teacher and student top-$k$ sets as


\begin{equation}
S_{T,n}^{k}
= \mathrm{TopK}_{k}\left(\pi_T(\cdot \mid h_n)\right),
\qquad
S_{\theta,n}^{k}
= \mathrm{TopK}_{k}\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid h_n)\right).
\label{eq:topk_sets}
\end{equation}

Given a distribution $q$ and a selected token set $S$, let $\tau_S$ denote the tail bucket containing all tokens in $V \setminus S$. This induces a coarsened distribution $q^{[S]}$ over $S \cup \{\tau_S\}$:

\begin{equation}
q^{[S]}(b \mid h_n)
=
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
q(b \mid h_n), & b \in S, \\
1 - \sum_{u \in S} q(u \mid h_n), & b = \tau_S.
\end{array}
\right.
\label{eq:tail_bucket_distribution}
\end{equation}

This construction preserves a coarse notion of the tail probability that is ignored by the top-$k$ token list. For example, with $k=10$, the first ten buckets are the ten selected tokens and the eleventh bucket is the total probability mass assigned to every other token. This has three benefits over renormalizing the selected tokens. First, it keeps the top-$k$ probabilities on their original scale, so a model that assigns $90\%$ probability mass to the selected tokens is not treated the same as a model that assigns only $40\%$ mass to them. Second, it allows the loss to penalize disagreements about how much probability should remain in the long tail, even though the individual tail tokens are not represented. Third, it avoids artificially inflating the selected tokens when the model is uncertain and spreads probability mass across many low-probability alternatives.

The standard top-$k$ forward KL uses the teacher top-$k$ set, because the expectation in the forward KL is taken under the teacher distribution. Both the teacher and student are evaluated on the same teacher-induced buckets $S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}$:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{k}
(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:topk_forward_kl}
\end{equation}

Analogously, the standard top-$k$ reverse KL uses the student top-$k$ set, because the expectation in the reverse KL is taken under the student distribution. The teacher is evaluated on the same student-induced buckets $S_{\theta,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{\theta,n}^{k}}\}$:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{k}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{\theta,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{\theta,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_\theta^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_T^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:topk_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/topk_forward_vs_reverse_kl.png}
    \caption{Top-$K$ approximation of the forward and reverse KL. Instead of summing over the full vocabulary $V$, the $K$ tokens with the highest teacher (forward) or student (reverse) probability are retained explicitly and the remaining vocabulary is grouped into a tail bucket.}
    \label{fig:topk_forward_vs_reverse_kl}
\end{figure}

For reasons explained by the support metrics in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}, we also use a variant that keeps the reverse-KL direction but selects the top-$k$ support from the teacher. We call this loss the \emph{Teacher-Support Reverse KL} (TS-RKL), because it uses the student-to-teacher KL ratio on the teacher-selected buckets:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{TS\mbox{-}RKL}}^{k}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:teacher_support_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

TS-RKL is not the standard top-$k$ reverse KL in Equation~\ref{eq:topk_reverse_kl}, because the explicit token buckets are chosen by the teacher rather than the student. The objective is still reverse KL in direction: the log-ratio is $\log \pi_\theta - \log \pi_T$ and the expectation is over the student distribution on the teacher-induced buckets. The difference is that the approximation focuses the comparison on tokens the teacher considers plausible while still preserving one aggregate bucket for the remaining tail mass.

\section{Privileged Context}

Privileged context is additional information that is available to the teacher during training but will not be available to the student at inference time. Following the privileged information distillation setting  \cite{shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026, hubotterReinforcementLearningSelfDistillation2026, penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026, zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026}, the key asymmetry is that the teacher is conditioned on this extra information, while the student is not. Following the notation of Section~\ref{sec:distillation_formalism}, let $c$ denote the privileged context associated with a prompt $x$. This context can take different forms, such as a reference solution, a successful action trace, tool calls, or a hint extracted from a stronger model. The student policy remains

\begin{equation}
\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n}),
\end{equation}

while the privileged teacher policy is evaluated as

\begin{equation}
\pi_T(\cdot \mid x, c, y_{<n}).
\end{equation}

The goal is therefore not to teach the student to depend on $c$, but to use $c$ to construct a more informative teacher signal during training. At inference time, the student still acts according to $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$.

We can express privileged-context distillation by replacing the teacher distribution in the KL objective with the context-conditioned teacher. For an on-policy rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$, the reverse-KL form becomes

\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_\theta(v \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})}
{\pi_T(v \mid x, c, \hat{y}_{<n})},
\label{eq:privileged_context_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

where $\pi_T^c$ denotes the teacher distribution conditioned on the privileged context $c$. The corresponding training objective is

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PCD}}(\theta)
=
\mathrm{E}_{(x,c) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{PI}}}
\left[
\mathrm{E}_{\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}
\left[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
\right]
\right],
\label{eq:privileged_context_distillation}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{PI}}$ is a dataset of prompts paired with privileged context. This objective keeps the rollout distribution on-policy with respect to the student, but computes the teacher probabilities using the stronger context $(x,c)$. In the self-distillation case, the teacher and student can share parameters and differ only in their input context. In our more general notation and in this thesis, $\pi_T$ is a separate teacher model. The defining property of privileged context is that only the teacher receives $c$.

As in Equation~\ref{eq:sampled_token_reverse_kl}, a top-1 sampled-token approximation keeps only the token actually produced by the student:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, c, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:privileged_context_sampled_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

This formulation makes the role of privileged context explicit. The sampled token and student probability are computed without access to $c$, while the teacher probability is computed with access to $c$. The loss therefore transfers the behavior induced by the privileged context into a student policy that must operate without it.

\section{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Besides the loss functions we track throughout training, we use additional metrics to measure how much teacher signal is visible to the student and how much of that signal is likely to be learnable. Unless stated otherwise, all token-level metrics are computed at a fixed prefix $h_n = (x,y_{<n})$, with student distribution $\pi(v)=\pi_\theta(v\mid h_n)$ and teacher distribution $q(v)=\pi_T(v\mid h_n)$. For a context-conditioned teacher, $q(v)$ is replaced by $\pi_T(v\mid x,c,y_{<n})$.

\textbf{Top-$K$ sets and overlap.}
For any distribution $r$ over the vocabulary, define
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{TopK}_K(r)
= \{ \text{the } K \text{ tokens with largest } r(v) \}.
\end{equation}
We write $T_K=\mathrm{TopK}_K(q)$ for the teacher top-$K$ set and $S_M=\mathrm{TopK}_M(\pi)$ for the student top-$M$ set. The top-$M$/top-$K$ overlap count and Jaccard overlap are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{overlap\_count}@M,K
= |S_M \cap T_K|,
\qquad
\mathrm{overlap\_jaccard}@M,K
=
\frac{|S_M \cap T_K|}{|S_M \cup T_K|}.
\label{eq:topmk_overlap}
\end{equation}
The top-10 Jaccard metric in the diagnostics is the special case $M=K=10$. We also track whether the student top-1 token is in $T_K$ and whether the teacher top-1 token is in $S_M$.

\textbf{Mass coverage.}
Set overlap alone ignores how probability mass is distributed inside the sets. The main reverse-KL support metric is the amount of student probability mass covered by the teacher top-$K$ support:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{student\_mass\_in\_teacher\_topK}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K}\pi(v),
\qquad
\mathrm{student\_mass\_outside\_teacher\_topK}@K
=
1-\sum_{v\in T_K}\pi(v).
\label{eq:student_mass_teacher_topk}
\end{equation}
When comparing the student top-$M$ set against the teacher top-$K$ set, we also use the mass-covered overlap
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{student\_topM\_mass\_in\_teacher\_topK}@M,K
=
\sum_{v\in S_M\cap T_K}\pi(v),
\label{eq:student_topm_mass_teacher_topk}
\end{equation}
and the corresponding teacher mass on student top-$M$ tokens,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{teacher\_topK\_mass\_on\_student\_topM}@M,K
=
\sum_{v\in S_M\cap T_K}q(v).
\label{eq:teacher_topk_mass_student_topm}
\end{equation}
These mass-weighted metrics are central for reverse KL because reverse KL is an expectation under the student distribution.

\textbf{Top-10 conditional Jensen-Shannon divergence.}
For two teacher distributions $q_A$ and $q_B$, we compare their returned top-10 distributions by first forming $U=T_{10}^{A}\cup T_{10}^{B}$. Each distribution is restricted to its own top-10 set, missing tokens in $U$ receive a small probability $\epsilon$ (default $10^{-12}$), and the result is renormalized over $U$. If these conditional distributions are $\widetilde{q}_A$ and $\widetilde{q}_B$, then
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{JS}_{10}(A,B)
=
\frac{1}{2}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde{q}_A\|m)
+
\frac{1}{2}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde{q}_B\|m),
\qquad
m=\frac{1}{2}(\widetilde{q}_A+\widetilde{q}_B).
\label{eq:top10_conditional_js}
\end{equation}
This is a top-10-conditional JS divergence, not a full-distribution JS divergence.

\textbf{Top-1 agreement and change rates.}
For any two teacher conditions $A$ and $B$ evaluated on the same prefix, let $a_A$ and $a_B$ denote their top-1 tokens. The top-1 agreement and change rates are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{same\_top1\_rate}(A,B)
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_A=a_B\}\right],
\qquad
\mathrm{different\_top1\_rate}(A,B)
=
1-\mathrm{same\_top1\_rate}(A,B).
\end{equation}
The prompt-sensitivity metric $\mathrm{top1\_change\_rate}_{A,B}$ is the same quantity as $\mathrm{different\_top1\_rate}(A,B)$. For three prompt levels $L1,L2,L3$, we also compute
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{all\_same\_top1\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_{L1}=a_{L2}=a_{L3}\}\right],
\qquad
\mathrm{any\_prompt\_changes\_top1\_rate}
=
1-\mathrm{all\_same\_top1\_rate}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Reachability and student support.}
For a teacher token $v$, the student negative log-likelihood is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v)=-\log \pi(v).
\end{equation}
The student rank of $v$ is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{rank}_{S}(v)
=
1+\left|\{u\in V:\pi(u)>\pi(v)\}\right|.
\end{equation}
We use the configurable default bands from the diagnostics:
\begin{equation}
\begin{array}{lll}
\text{too close} & \text{if} & \mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v)<0.25,\\
\text{reachable} & \text{if} & 0.25\leq \mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v)\leq 8.0,\\
\text{too far} & \text{if} & \mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v)>8.0.
\end{array}
\label{eq:reachability_bands}
\end{equation}
For teacher top-$K$ diagnostics that normalize over the returned teacher set, define
\begin{equation}
q_K^{\mathrm{cond}}(v)
=
\frac{q(v)}{\sum_{u\in T_K}q(u)}
\quad\text{for }v\in T_K.
\end{equation}
The teacher top-$K$ reachable mass, expected student NLL, and minimum student NLL are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{teacher\_topK\_reachable\_mass}
=
\sum_{v\in T_K}q_K^{\mathrm{cond}}(v)\mathbf{1}\{v\text{ is reachable}\},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{teacher\_topK\_expected\_student\_NLL}
=
\sum_{v\in T_K}q_K^{\mathrm{cond}}(v)\mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v),
\qquad
\mathrm{teacher\_topK\_min\_student\_NLL}
=
\min_{v\in T_K}\mathrm{NLL}_{S}(v).
\end{equation}
We say that the teacher top-$K$ set contains a reachable alternative when the teacher top-1 token is too far but at least one token in $T_K$ is reachable.

\textbf{Student entropy and OPD utility.}
The full student entropy and its normalized version are
\begin{equation}
H(\pi)=-\sum_{v\in V}\pi(v)\log \pi(v),
\qquad
H_{\mathrm{norm}}(\pi)=\frac{H(\pi)}{\log |V|}.
\end{equation}
For a teacher top-1 token $a_T$, let $d_T=\mathrm{NLL}_{S}(a_T)$. Using student-specific calibration quantiles, we normalize the teacher-token NLL and student entropy as
\begin{equation}
\widehat{d}
=
\mathrm{clip}\left(
\frac{d_T-q05_{\mathrm{nll}}}{q95_{\mathrm{nll}}-q05_{\mathrm{nll}}+\epsilon},0,1
\right),
\qquad
\widehat{h}
=
\mathrm{clip}\left(
\frac{H_{\mathrm{norm}}-q05_{\mathrm{entropy}}}{q95_{\mathrm{entropy}}-q05_{\mathrm{entropy}}+\epsilon},0,1
\right).
\end{equation}
The soft-OR utility proxy is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{soft\_or}
=
\widehat{h}+\widehat{d}-\widehat{h}\widehat{d},
\qquad
\mathrm{reachable\_soft\_or}
=
\mathrm{soft\_or}\cdot \mathbf{1}\{a_T\text{ is reachable}\}.
\end{equation}
The Q-bucket proxy separates high- and low-entropy/high- and low-NLL cases: Q1 has $\widehat{h}\geq0.65$ and $\widehat{d}\geq0.65$; Q2 has $\widehat{h}\geq0.65$ and $\widehat{d}<0.35$; Q3 has $\widehat{h}<0.35$ and $\widehat{d}\geq0.65$; Q4 has $\widehat{h}<0.35$ and $\widehat{d}<0.35$.

\textbf{Top-1 bottleneck and extractability.}
For a larger teacher $L$ and a smaller teacher $M$, we compute prefix-averaged rates with the indicator function $\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{hard\_label\_same\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_L=a_M\}\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{top10\_diff\_hidden\_by\_top1\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_L=a_M\ \text{and}\ \mathrm{JS}_{10}(L,M)\geq0.10\}\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{larger\_teacher\_unreachable\_top1\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_L\neq a_M\ \text{and}\ a_L\text{ is too far}\}\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{larger\_teacher\_reachable\_difference\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_L\neq a_M\ \text{and}\ a_L\text{ is reachable}\}\right].
\end{equation}
The top-$K$ rescue rate is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{topK\_rescue\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[
\mathbf{1}\{
(a_L\text{ is too far}\ \text{or}\ a_L=a_M)
\quad \text{and}\quad
\exists v\in T_K^{L}: v\neq a_S,\ v\text{ is reachable}
\}
\right],
\label{eq:topk_rescue_rate}
\end{equation}
where $a_S$ is the student top-1 token. The top-1 extractability proxy is the larger-teacher reachable-difference rate, and the top-$K$ extractability proxy adds the top-$K$ rescue rate.

\textbf{Bucketed reverse-KL support.}
When returned teacher log probabilities are full-distribution probabilities rather than top-$K$-renormalized probabilities, we compute a coarse reverse-KL diagnostic over the teacher top-$K$ partition $T_K\cup\{\mathrm{OTHER}\}$:
\begin{equation}
q_K(v)=q(v),\quad
\pi_K(v)=\pi(v)
\quad\text{for }v\in T_K,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
q_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
=
\max\left(\epsilon,1-\sum_{v\in T_K}q(v)\right),
\qquad
\pi_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
=
\max\left(\epsilon,1-\sum_{v\in T_K}\pi(v)\right).
\end{equation}
The bucketed reverse KL is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{bucketed\_RKL}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K}\pi_K(v)\left[\log \pi_K(v)-\log q_K(v)\right]
+
\pi_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
\left[
\log \pi_K(\mathrm{OTHER})-\log q_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
\right].
\label{eq:bucketed_rkl_metric}
\end{equation}
We report the visible-token contribution,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{visible\_token\_rkl\_contribution}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K}\pi_K(v)\left[\log \pi_K(v)-\log q_K(v)\right],
\end{equation}
the OTHER-bucket contribution,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{other\_bucket\_rkl\_contribution}@K
=
\pi_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
\left[
\log \pi_K(\mathrm{OTHER})-\log q_K(\mathrm{OTHER})
\right],
\end{equation}
and the fraction of bucketed reverse KL from the OTHER bucket,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{other\_bucket\_fraction\_of\_bucketed\_RKL}@K
=
\frac{\mathrm{other\_bucket\_rkl\_contribution}@K}
{\max(\epsilon,\mathrm{bucketed\_RKL}@K)}.
\end{equation}
This is a coarse partition diagnostic, not exact full-vocabulary reverse KL.

\textbf{Common-partition teacher comparison.}
For teacher comparisons, especially 30B versus 235B, let
\begin{equation}
U_K=T_K^{A}\cup T_K^{B}.
\end{equation}
Because teacher log probabilities may only be available for returned top-$N$ tokens, we separately track the fraction of $U_K$ scored by both teachers, student mass on $U_K$, student mass on tokens scored by both teachers, and student mass on tokens visible to only one teacher. On the subset $B_K\subseteq U_K$ scored by both teachers, the student-mass-weighted advantage of teacher $B$ over teacher $A$ is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{advantage}_{B/A}@K
=
\sum_{v\in B_K}\pi(v)\left[\log q_B(v)-\log q_A(v)\right],
\end{equation}
with normalized version
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{normalized\_advantage}_{B/A}@K
=
\frac{\sum_{v\in B_K}\pi(v)\left[\log q_B(v)-\log q_A(v)\right]}
{\max\left(\epsilon,\sum_{v\in B_K}\pi(v)\right)}.
\end{equation}
For $B=$235B and $A=$30B, a positive value means that the larger teacher assigns higher log probability on the visible student-supported subset.

\textbf{Privileged-context support effect.}
Let $T_K^{c}$ denote the teacher top-$K$ set with privileged context and $T_K^{0}$ the corresponding set without privileged context. The reverse-KL support effect of privileged context is
\begin{equation}
\Delta \mathrm{coverage}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K^{c}}\pi(v)
-
\sum_{v\in T_K^{0}}\pi(v).
\end{equation}
For bucketed reverse KL, we use
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathrm{bucketed\_RKL}@K
=
\mathrm{bucketed\_RKL}_{\mathrm{without\ PC}}@K
-
\mathrm{bucketed\_RKL}_{\mathrm{with\ PC}}@K,
\end{equation}
so positive values mean that privileged context lowers the coarse reverse-KL mismatch. We also track
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{student\_mass\_added\_by\_PC}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K^{c}\setminus T_K^{0}}\pi(v),
\qquad
\mathrm{student\_mass\_removed\_by\_PC}@K
=
\sum_{v\in T_K^{0}\setminus T_K^{c}}\pi(v).
\end{equation}
For the top-1 privileged-context influence metrics, let $a_c$ and $a_0$ be the teacher top-1 tokens with and without privileged context. We compute
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{context\_top1\_change\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_c\neq a_0\}\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{context\_changes\_to\_reachable\_token\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_c\neq a_0\ \text{and}\ a_c\text{ is reachable}\}\right],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{context\_changes\_to\_too\_far\_token\_rate}
=
\mathrm{mean}\left[\mathbf{1}\{a_c\neq a_0\ \text{and}\ a_c\text{ is too far}\}\right].
\end{equation}
The context top-10 Jaccard and conditional JS metrics are computed by applying Equations~\ref{eq:topmk_overlap} and~\ref{eq:top10_conditional_js} to the with-context and without-context teacher distributions.

\textbf{Reverse-KL-visible rescue.}
The reverse-KL-visible rescue metric is the mass-weighted version of the top-$K$ rescue diagnostic. For student top-1 token $a_S$, define
\begin{equation}
R_K
=
\{v\in T_K : v\neq a_S\ \text{and}\ v\text{ is reachable}\}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{rkl\_visible\_rescue\_mass}@K
=
\sum_{v\in R_K}\pi(v),
\qquad
\mathrm{teacher\_mass\_on\_rescue\_tokens}@K
=
\sum_{v\in R_K}q(v),
\qquad
\mathrm{rescue\_token\_count}@K
=
|R_K|.
\end{equation}
We mark a prefix as having reverse-KL-visible rescue when the rescue mass exceeds a chosen threshold.

\textbf{Prompt support sensitivity and top-$K$ sufficiency.}
For two teacher prompts $p$ and $p'$, prompt support sensitivity is measured by the absolute coverage change
\begin{equation}
\left|
\sum_{v\in T_K^{p}}\pi(v)
-
\sum_{v\in T_K^{p'}}\pi(v)
\right|,
\end{equation}
the Jaccard overlap between $T_K^p$ and $T_K^{p'}$, the student mass on tokens that enter or leave $T_K$, and the rate at which the absolute coverage change is at least $0.01$. To assess whether a chosen top-$K$ is large enough, we compute
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{coverage\_gain}_{10\rightarrow50}
=
\mathrm{coverage}@50-\mathrm{coverage}@10,
\qquad
\mathrm{coverage\_gain}_{50\rightarrow100}
=
\mathrm{coverage}@100-\mathrm{coverage}@50,
\end{equation}
with analogous gains for bucketed reverse KL and reverse-KL-visible rescue mass. If a larger returned top-$N$ set is available, we also compute
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{coverage\_gap}_{100\rightarrow N}
=
\mathrm{student\_mass\_in\_teacher\_returned\_topN}
-
\mathrm{student\_mass\_in\_teacher\_top100}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Gap Recovery Rate.}
We are not only interested in the final performance of the student, but also in how much of the teacher performance the student recovers. For that purpose, we use the gap recovery rate \cite{liRethinkingOnPolicyDistillation2026}:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{GapRecovery}
=
\frac{\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{After}}-\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}}
{\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Teacher}}-\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}$ and $\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{After}}$ are the student performance before and after training, respectively.
