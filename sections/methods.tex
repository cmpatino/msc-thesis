\chapter{Methods}

This chapter defines the methodological framework used to study distillation under extreme teacher--student capacity gaps. We first introduce the distillation objectives used throughout the thesis, distinguishing off-policy distillation from on-policy distillation and forward KL from reverse KL. We then formalize two ways of changing the teacher signal: privileged context, where additional information is visible only to the teacher during scoring, and context distillation, where the same information can also shape the student's rollout distribution. Finally, we introduce the top-$K$ approximation used to expose more of the teacher distribution and define the metrics used to evaluate whether additional teacher information becomes useful student behavior.

\section{Distillation Formalism} \label{sec:distillation_formalism}

Following the notation of on-policy generalized knowledge distillation \cite{agarwalOnPolicyDistillationLanguage2024}, let $x$ denote a prompt, let $V$ denote the vocabulary, and let $y = (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_{L_y})$ denote an output sequence. The prefix before token $y_n$ is $y_{<n} = (y_1, \ldots, y_{n-1})$. We write the student policy as $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$ and the teacher policy as $\pi_T(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$, both of which define next-token distributions over $V$.

The simplest distillation objective is SFT, which treats a target sequence as a hard label $y_n$ and trains the student with cross-entropy. If $\mathcal{D}$ is a dataset of prompt--completion pairs, where the completions can be human-written or sampled from the teacher, the loss is

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(\theta)
= \mathrm{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}
\left[
- \sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\log \pi_\theta(y_n \mid x, y_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:xentropy}
\end{equation}

A richer form of distillation uses the full teacher distribution over the vocabulary instead of using the token sampled by the teacher. For a fixed prompt and completion, the token-level forward KL from teacher to student is

\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_y} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})}
{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})}.
\label{eq:forward_kl}
\end{equation}
%
The reverse KL swaps the order of the two distributions:
%
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_y} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, y_{<n})}
{\pi_T(v \mid x, y_{<n})}.
\label{eq:reverse_kl}
\end{equation}
%
The distinction is important because KL divergence is asymmetric. Forward KL (FKL) averages the log-ratio under the teacher distribution, so it penalizes the student for failing to place probability mass on tokens the teacher considers likely. This makes it a mass-covering objective. Reverse KL (RKL) averages under the student distribution, so it penalizes tokens the student samples when the teacher assigns them low probability. This makes it more mode-seeking and can be useful when a lower-capacity student cannot match the full teacher distribution.

We show the distinction graphically in Figure~\ref{fig:toy_forward_vs_reverse} using a plot from \cite{guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026} that shows how the forward and reverse KL fit a Gaussian mixture distribution with a single Gaussian distribution. The reverse KL seeks the major mode of the mixture while the forward KL tries to fit all three.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/forward_vs_reverse.pdf}
    \caption{Image from \cite{guMiniLLMOnPolicyDistillation2026} showing the difference of using forward and reverse KL to fit a mixture of three Gaussians. The reverse KL tries to fit the major mode while the forward KL tries to approximate the three modes.}
    \label{fig:toy_forward_vs_reverse}
\end{figure}

On top of the distinction between forward and reverse KL, it is important to distinguish where we sample the rollouts $y$ we use for training. Off-policy distillation samples the rollouts from a teacher policy $y \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$ while OPD samples completions from the student and then compares teacher and student distributions on the prefixes that the student actually visits. We illustrate both settings in Figure~\ref{fig:off_vs_on_policy}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/off_vs_on_policy_distillation.png}
    \caption{Off-policy distillation generates completions from the teacher and trains the student on them. On-policy distillation generates completions from the student and scores them with both models.}
    \label{fig:off_vs_on_policy}
\end{figure}

The on-policy objective is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OPD}}(\theta)
= 
\mathrm{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}
\left[
D(\pi_T, \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
\right],
\label{eq:on_policy_distillation}
\end{equation}
%
where $D$ can be instantiated as the forward KL in Equation~\ref{eq:forward_kl}, the reverse KL in Equation~\ref{eq:reverse_kl}, or another token-level divergence. We can define off-policy distillation analogously by changing $y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ for $y \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$.

In practice, computing the full sums over $V$ can be expensive. A common solution is to only use one log-probability instead of the distribution over the full vocabulary. The sampled-token reverse-KL formulation used in on-policy distillation keeps only the realized token at each position of a student rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$. The resulting top-1 sampled-token estimate is

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(\hat{y} \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:sampled_token_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}
%
This is the sampled-token analogue of Equation~\ref{eq:reverse_kl}. Rather than matching the full next-token distributions, it scores the token actually produced by the student under both policies.

We can write an analogous formulation for Equation~\ref{eq:forward_kl}, where we only use the realized token at each position of a teacher rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x)$. Additionally, we use the forward instead of the reverse KL to avoid optimizing a biased surrogate or using importance sampling to correct the off-policy nature of the data.

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(\hat{y} \mid x)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl}
\end{equation}
%
The formulation in Equation~\ref{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl} is equivalent to the soft version of SFT, because we are including the log probability of the token sampled by the teacher instead of excluding it and using it as a hard target as in Equation~\ref{eq:xentropy}.

Regardless of the loss function we choose, we can specify what the teacher and student see during training to change the behavior of the trained student.

\section{Privileged Context} \label{sec: methods_pc}

Privileged context is additional information that is available to the teacher during training but will not be available to the student at inference time. Following the privileged information distillation setting  \cite{shenfeldSelfDistillationEnablesContinual2026, hubotterReinforcementLearningSelfDistillation2026, penalozaPrivilegedInformationDistillation2026, zhaoSelfDistilledReasonerOnPolicy2026}, the key asymmetry is that the teacher is conditioned on this extra information, while the student is not. Using the notation of Section~\ref{sec:distillation_formalism}, let $c$ denote the privileged context associated with a prompt $x$. This context can take different forms, such as a reference solution, a successful action trace, tool calls, or a hint extracted from a stronger model. The student policy remains

\begin{equation}
\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n}),
\end{equation}
%
while the privileged teacher policy is evaluated as
%
\begin{equation}
\pi_T(\cdot \mid x, c, y_{<n}).
\end{equation}
%
The goal is therefore not to teach the student to depend on $c$, but to use $c$ to construct a more informative teacher signal during training. At inference time, the student still acts according to $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, y_{<n})$.

We can express privileged-context distillation by replacing the teacher distribution in the KL objective with the context-conditioned teacher. For an on-policy rollout $\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$, the reverse-KL form becomes
%
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}} \sum_{v \in V}
\pi_\theta(v \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta(v \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})}
{\pi_T(v \mid x, c, \hat{y}_{<n})},
\label{eq:privileged_context_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}
%
where $\pi_T^c$ denotes the teacher distribution conditioned on the privileged context $c$. The corresponding training objective is
%
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PCD}}(\theta)
=
\mathrm{E}_{(x,c) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{PI}}}
\left[
\mathrm{E}_{\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)}
\left[
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
\right]
\right],
\label{eq:privileged_context_distillation}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{PI}}$ is a dataset of prompts paired with privileged context. This objective keeps the rollout distribution on-policy with respect to the student, but computes the teacher probabilities using the stronger context $(x,c)$. In the self-distillation case, the teacher and student can share parameters and differ only in their input context. In our more general notation and in this thesis, $\pi_T$ is a separate teacher model. The defining property of privileged context is that only the teacher receives $c$.

As in Equation~\ref{eq:sampled_token_reverse_kl}, a top-1 sampled-token approximation keeps only the token actually produced by the student:
%
\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{\mathrm{top}\mbox{-}1}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T^c)(\hat{y} \mid x, c)
= \sum_{n=1}^{L_{\hat{y}}}
\left[
\log \pi_\theta(\hat{y}_n \mid x, \hat{y}_{<n})
- \log \pi_T(\hat{y}_n \mid x, c, \hat{y}_{<n})
\right].
\label{eq:privileged_context_sampled_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}
%
This formulation makes the role of privileged context explicit. The sampled token and student probability are computed without access to $c$, while the teacher probability is computed with access to $c$. The loss therefore transfers the behavior induced by the privileged context into a student policy that must operate without it.

In the teacher-assisted variant used later in the experiments, the privileged context $c$ is a rollout generated by a larger teacher for the same prompt. The scoring teacher is smaller than this larger teacher but larger or more capable than the student. The student does not see the larger teacher's rollout; it only receives token-level feedback from the scoring teacher conditioned on that rollout. This makes the setup a single-run analogue of teacher-assistant distillation: the intermediate teacher is used at scoring time rather than trained as a separate model.

\section{Context Distillation} \label{sec: methods_cd}

The privileged-context formulation above gives one use of the context $c$: the teacher sees $c$, the student does not, and the loss transfers the behavior induced by $c$ through teacher log-probabilities. More generally, context distillation refers to training procedures in which $c$ contains an instruction or other conditioning signal whose effect we want the trained student to incorporate. The possible mechanisms depend on whether the rollout distribution is off-policy or on-policy.

In off-policy distillation, the rollouts are generated before training by the teacher. Since the student does not generate the training states, the only way for the context to affect training is through the teacher prompt used to produce the completions. The context-conditioned rollout is

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}^c \sim \pi_T(\cdot \mid x,c),
\end{equation}
%
and the student is trained to imitate this fixed completion. This is the same mechanism as SFT on teacher-generated traces in Equation~\ref{eq:xentropy}, except that the traces come from the context-conditioned teacher. If teacher log-probabilities are also stored, the sampled-token forward-KL form from Equation~\ref{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl} can be used instead. In either case, off-policy context distillation transfers $c$ only through the teacher rollout and the student learns to mimic outputs that were generated under the instruction in $c$.

On-policy distillation has two distinct ways to introduce the same context. The first is the privileged-context setting from Section~\ref{sec: methods_pc}. The student generates an ordinary on-policy rollout

\begin{equation}
\hat{y} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x),
\end{equation}
%
but the teacher scores each prefix with access to $c$. Using the top-1 reverse-KL approximation, this corresponds to the privileged-context objective in Equation~\ref{eq:privileged_context_sampled_reverse_kl}.
%
Here $c$ is distilled through the teacher probabilities. The student never observes the context directly, but it is updated toward token choices that the teacher prefers when the teacher is conditioned on $c$.

The second on-policy mechanism is to give the context directly to the student during rollout generation. In this case the rollout distribution itself becomes context-conditioned:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}^c \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x,c).
\end{equation}
%
The same on-policy divergence from Equation~\ref{eq:on_policy_distillation} can then be applied to the tokens produced by this context-aware student, with the teacher evaluated on the same context-conditioned prefixes. In this variant, $c$ changes the states visited during training because the student generates its own rollouts while aware of the context. This differs from privileged-context distillation, where the rollout distribution is still $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ and only the teacher scores with $c$. Both on-policy variants can produce a trained student whose behavior reflects the instruction in $c$ without incorporating the instruction explicitly during inference.

\section{Top-K KL} \label{sec: top-k_kl}

The KL objectives in Equations~\ref{eq:forward_kl} and~\ref{eq:reverse_kl} require summing over the full vocabulary at every token position. This is expensive for large language models, because the vocabulary can contain hundreds of thousands of tokens. The other end of the spectrum is only using the one log-probability as shown in Equations~\ref{eq:sampled_token_reverse_kl} and \ref{eq:sampled_token_forward_kl}. A practical approximation that balances these approaches is to keep the $k$ tokens with the largest log-probabilities under one of the two policies. However, we do not renormalize the retained log-probabilities over only those $k$ tokens. Renormalization would discard how much probability mass the model assigns to the rest of the vocabulary. Instead, we group all tokens outside the selected top-$k$ set into a single tail bucket.

For compactness, write $h_n = (x, y_{<n})$. For each prefix, define the teacher and student top-$k$ sets as

\begin{equation}
S_{T,n}^{k}
= \mathrm{TopK}_{k}\left(\pi_T(\cdot \mid h_n)\right),
\qquad
S_{\theta,n}^{k}
= \mathrm{TopK}_{k}\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid h_n)\right).
\label{eq:topk_sets}
\end{equation}
%
Given a distribution $q$ and a selected token set $S$, let $\tau_S$ denote the tail bucket containing all tokens in $V \setminus S$. This induces a coarsened distribution $q^{[S]}$ over $S \cup \{\tau_S\}$:
%
\begin{equation}
q^{[S]}(b \mid h_n)
=
\left\{
\begin{array}{ll}
q(b \mid h_n), & b \in S, \\
1 - \sum_{u \in S} q(u \mid h_n), & b = \tau_S.
\end{array}
\right.
\label{eq:tail_bucket_distribution}
\end{equation}
%
This construction preserves a coarse notion of the tail probability that is ignored by the top-$k$ token list. For example, with $k=10$, the first ten buckets are the ten selected tokens and the eleventh bucket is the total probability mass assigned to every other token. This has three benefits over renormalizing the selected tokens. First, it keeps the top-$k$ probabilities on their original scale, so a model that assigns $90\%$ probability mass to the selected tokens is not treated the same as a model that assigns only $40\%$ mass to them. Second, it allows the loss to penalize disagreements about how much probability should remain in the long tail, even though the individual tail tokens are not represented. Third, it avoids artificially inflating the selected tokens when the model is uncertain and spreads probability mass across many low-probability alternatives.

The standard top-$k$ forward KL uses the teacher top-$k$ set, because the expectation in the forward KL is taken under the teacher distribution. Both the teacher and student are evaluated on the same teacher-induced buckets $S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}$:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{k}
(\pi_T \| \pi_\theta)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:topk_forward_kl}
\end{equation}
%
Analogously, the standard top-$k$ reverse KL uses the student top-$k$ set, because the expectation in the reverse KL is taken under the student distribution. The teacher is evaluated on the same student-induced buckets $S_{\theta,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{\theta,n}^{k}}\}$:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{KL}}^{k}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{\theta,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{\theta,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_\theta^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_T^{[S_{\theta,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:topk_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/topk_forward_vs_reverse_kl.png}
    \caption{Top-$K$ approximation of the forward and reverse KL. Instead of summing over the full vocabulary $V$, the $K$ tokens with the highest teacher (forward) or student (reverse) probability are retained explicitly and the remaining vocabulary is grouped into a tail bucket.}
    \label{fig:topk_forward_vs_reverse_kl}
\end{figure}

For reasons explained in Section \ref{sec: inference_engine}, we also use a variant that keeps the reverse-KL direction but selects the top-$k$ support from the teacher. We call this loss the \emph{Teacher-Support Reverse KL} (TS-RKL), because it uses the student-to-teacher KL ratio on the teacher-selected buckets:

\begin{equation}
\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{TS\mbox{-}RKL}}^{k}
(\pi_\theta \| \pi_T)(y \mid x)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{L_y}
\sum_{b \in S_{T,n}^{k} \cup \{\tau_{S_{T,n}^{k}}\}}
\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)
\log
\frac{\pi_\theta^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}
{\pi_T^{[S_{T,n}^{k}]}(b \mid h_n)}.
\label{eq:teacher_support_reverse_kl}
\end{equation}
%
TS-RKL is not the standard top-$k$ reverse KL in Equation~\ref{eq:topk_reverse_kl}, because the explicit token buckets are chosen by the teacher rather than the student. The objective is still reverse KL in direction: the log-ratio is $\log \pi_\theta - \log \pi_T$ and the expectation is over the student distribution on the teacher-induced buckets.

\section{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\textbf{Top-$k$ Support Coverage.}
A teacher-support top-$k$ reverse-KL objective can only provide token-level supervision on the tokens in the teacher's top-$k$ set. Student probability mass placed on tokens outside that set is therefore not supervised at the token level. We measure how much of the student's next-token distribution is spanned by the teacher's top-$k$ support. Let $\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{eval}}$ be the set of prefixes used for evaluation, with each prefix written as $h_n=(x,y_{<n})$. Using the teacher top-$k$ set $S_{T,n}^{k}$ from Equation~\ref{eq:topk_sets}, we define the top-$k$ support coverage as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Coverage}_{k}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{eval}}|}
\sum_{h_n \in \mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{eval}}}
\sum_{v \in S_{T,n}^{k}}
\pi_\theta(v \mid h_n).
\label{eq:topk_support_coverage}
\end{equation}
This is the student probability mass that falls inside the teacher's top-$k$, averaged over prefixes. Its complement $1-\mathrm{Coverage}_{k}$ is the student mass lying outside the teacher's top-$k$, i.e. the fraction of the student's distribution that a teacher top-$k$ support cannot supervise at the token level.

\noindent\textbf{Gap Recovery.}
We are not only interested in the final performance of the student, but also in how much of the teacher performance the student recovers. Following \cite{liRethinkingOnPolicyDistillation2026}, we measure the recovered teacher-student gap as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{GapRecovery}
=
\frac{\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{After}}-\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}}
{\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Teacher}}-\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{Before}}$ and $\mathrm{Acc}_{\mathrm{After}}$ are the student performance before and after training, respectively.
