\begin{abstract}
Distillation is widely used to make large language model capabilities cheaper to deploy, and has applications in domain-specific assistants, task-focused reasoners, and other systems where a small specialized model is preferable to serving a much larger general model. \cs{You never explain the distillation problem.} Recent work has addressed the distillation problem by training students from teacher-generated data, introducing intermediate or adaptive distillation targets, and using on-policy distillation in which a teacher scores rollouts sampled from the current student. Unfortunately, these approaches leave open how distillation behaves when the teacher is hundreds of times larger than the student, a regime where the strongest teachers can also produce supervision that is too complex for the student to learn effectively.

This thesis studies extreme capacity gaps between teachers and students directly and focuses on the on-policy distillation setting. On-policy distillation reduces the mismatch between training and inference-time behavior, but it does not by itself solve the problem that small students may receive teacher signals that are too verbose, too sharp, or too specialized to internalize safely. Consequently, we develop and evaluate on-policy distillation variants for mathematical reasoning, including privileged-context teacher scoring, top-$k$ teacher losses, and student-visible context distillation for encouraging concise reasoning. While promising, this regime is non-trivial because on-policy rollouts must be generated inside the training loop, large teachers must provide token-level feedback efficiently, and specialization can cause catastrophic forgetting outside the target domain.
